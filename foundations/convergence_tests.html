1.2. Convergence Results#

Next, we’ll get to some convergence concepts that will be useful throughout this course. These will often be conducted on series, which are basically sequential sums of sequences:

Definition 1.10 (Partial sum)

Let \(\mathcal A \subset \mathbb R\) be a set, and let \((a_n)\) be a sequence on \(\mathcal A\). A \(m\)-element partial sum of the sequence is:

\[\begin{split} s_m &= \sum_{n = 1}^m a_n \\ Test &= test\end{split}\]

Definition 1.11 (Partial sum)

Let \(\mathcal A \subset \mathbb R\) be a set, and let \((a_n)\) be a sequence on \(\mathcal A\). A \(m\)-element partial sum of the sequence is:

\[ Test &= test\]

This allows us to define a series:

Definition 1.12 (Series)

Let \(\mathcal A \subset \mathbb R\) be a set, and let \((a_n)\) be a sequence on \(\mathcal A\). A series on \(\mathcal A\) is the inifinte sum:

\[ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\]

With \(s_m = \sum_{n = 1}^m a_n\) the \(m^{th}\) partial sum, we define the sum of the series to be:

\[\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n = \lim_{m \rightarrow \infty} s_m\]

If the limit exists and is finite, we say that the series converges; otherwise, we say that the series diverges.

We’re going to be extremely interested in the behavior of these series and their behaviors. This means that we need some tools to easily let us determine for which particular sequences \((a_n)\) that the resulting series converges/diverges. First up is the simplest test, the comparison test:

Lemma 1.1 (Comparison Test)

Let \(\mathcal A \subset \mathbb R\), and let \((a_n)\) and \((b_n)\) be sequences on \(\mathcal A\) where all \(a_n, b_n \geq 0\). Then:

  1. If \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n\) is a convergent series and \(a_n \leq b_n\) for all \(n\), then \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty\) converges.

  2. If \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty b_n\) is a divergent series and \(a_n \geq b_n\) for all \(n\), then \(\sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n\) diverges.

When we have easy-to-use series that we already know behavior about, this can make proving convergence somewhat trivial. Let’s introduce the integral test, and we’ll see how the comparison test can help us out:

Lemma 1.2 (Integral Test)

Let \(\mathcal A \subset \mathbb R\), and let \((a_n)\) be a sequence on \(\mathcal A\). Let \(f : \mathbb R \rightarrow \mathbb R\) be a function, where in particular \(f(n) = a_n\). If \(f\) is positive, continuous, and decreasing, then both:

\[ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty a_n \text{ and }\int_1^\infty f(n)\text{d} n\]

converge or diverge.

And a quick example of the integral test and the comparison test in action:

Example 1.1

Let’s consider the series:

\[ \sum_{n = 2}^\infty \frac{1}{n(\log n)^s}\]

If \(s = 1\), let’s take a look at that integral. Notice that:

\[\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{n(\log n)}\text{d} n\]

When we choose \(u = \log n\), we obtain that \(\frac{\text{d}u}{\text{d}n} = \frac{1}{n}\), so \(\text{d}u = \frac{1}{n}\text{d}n\). Then our integral is:

\[\begin{split}\int_{\log 2}^\infty \frac{1}{u}\text{d} u &= \log(u) + C\big |_{u=\log 2}^\infty \\ &= \log(\log n) + C\big |_{n=2}^\infty = \infty\end{split}\]

so the series diverges.

When \(s < 1\), then \(\frac{1}{n (\log n)^s} > \frac{1}{n (\log n)} \geq 0\), so this series must diverge by Lemma 1.1.

When \(s > 1\), then we can’t just use the comparison test, so we need to calculate this integral out as well. This is because the terms of the sequence \(\frac{1}{n \log^s n}\) are not bigger than \(\frac{1}{n\log n}\). Using the same \(u\)-substitution as before, we get:

\[\begin{split}\int_2^\infty \frac{1}{n(\log n)^s}\text{d} n &= \int_2^\infty u^{-s}\text{d} u \\ &= \frac{u^{1 - s}}{1 - s} + C \big|_{u = \log 2}^\infty \\ &= \frac{(\log n)^{1 - s}}{1 - s} \big|_{ n = 2}^\infty \\ &= -\frac{1}{(s - 1)(\log n)^{s - 1}} \big|_{n = 2}^\infty \\ &= 0 + \frac{1}{(s - 1)\log(2)^{s - 1}}\end{split}\]

Which is finite (the integral is convergent). So our results are:

  1. If \(s \leq 1\), the series diverges.

  2. If \(s > 1\), the series converges.

Next up is probably the most important convergence result that you will want to know inside and out, the \(p\)-series convergence test:

Lemma 1.3 (\(p\)-series Convergence Test)

The \(p\)-series is given by, with \(p > 0\):

\[ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^p}\]

If \(p > 1\), then the series converges. If \(p \in (0, 1]\), then the series diverges.
